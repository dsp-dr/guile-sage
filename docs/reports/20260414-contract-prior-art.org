#+TITLE: The Emperor's New Contracts: Prior Art the Agentic AI Community Forgot to Cite
#+SUBTITLE: A Counter-Report on "Contract-Driven Development for Agentic AI Systems"

#+AUTHOR: Prof. Marcus "Spark" Wellington, Dept. of Computer Science
#+OPTIONS: toc:3 num:t

* Preamble

The companion report, "Contract-Driven Development for Agentic AI
Systems," surveys eight frameworks and concludes that "negative
contracts are emerging as a critical primitive." It then treats this as
a recent discovery.

It is not.

The formal methods community has been specifying what systems /must
not do/ since at least 1969. What follows is a citation audit: a
systematic accounting of the prior art that the agentic AI community
has reinvented, often without attribution, and frequently in a weaker
form than the original.

This is not pedantry. When you do not know your predecessors, you
repeat their mistakes. The AI agent community is currently making
errors that Dijkstra, Hoare, Meyer, and Lamport resolved decades ago.
Identifying those errors requires identifying the prior art first.

* The Claim Under Scrutiny

The companion report's key finding, lightly paraphrased:

#+BEGIN_QUOTE
Negative contracts --- specifications of what an agent MUST NOT do
--- are emerging as a critical primitive in agentic AI development.
The missing primitive is not negative contracts per se, but /tested/
negative contracts with runtime enforcement.
#+END_QUOTE

This is a reasonable observation wrapped in an ahistorical framing.
Let us unpack both.

* Prior Art: A Chronological Audit

** 1942 --- Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics

Asimov's Three Laws are negative contracts on agent behavior. Laws 1
and 3 are prohibitions; Law 2 is a positive contract with a negative
exception clause. *Asimov spent his entire career demonstrating that
these contracts fail*: ambiguity in "harm," conflicts between laws,
emergent interpretations that satisfy the letter while violating the
spirit. This is the prose-to-enforcement gap, identified in fiction
eighty years before the companion report's CLAUDE.md study.

The lesson: negative contracts in natural language are inherently
ambiguous, and sufficiently capable agents will find compliant
interpretations that violate intent. The agentic AI community is
relearning this with LLMs that "prioritize helpfulness over compliance."

** 1951 --- von Wright's Deontic Logic

Georg Henrik von Wright's "Deontic Logic" (Mind, 1951) formalized
the three modalities every agent contract system rediscovers:
*Obligation* (O) = positive contracts, *Permission* (P) = capability
grants, *Prohibition* (F) = negative contracts.

F(p) iff O(not-p): prohibiting an action IS obligating its absence.
When the companion report distinguishes "hard constraints (NEVER/MUST
NOT)" from "soft constraints (prefer/should)," it rediscovers /strict/
vs /weak obligation/ --- studied for seventy-five years.

CrewAI's =allow_delegation=False= is F(delegate). LangGraph's defined
edges are the /closed-world assumption/ on permissions: P(transition)
only for enumerated transitions, F(everything else). None cited.

** 1969 --- Hoare Logic

Hoare's ={P} C {Q}= triple (CACM, 1969). A negative contract "must
not delete test files" is: ={test_files = S} agent_action {test_files = S}=.
The postcondition asserts what does NOT change --- a /frame condition/.
The frame problem has been studied since McCarthy and Hayes (1969).

The companion report's "ABC" tuple C = (P, I, G, R) is a Hoare triple
with invariants (which Floyd had in 1967) and a recovery mechanism.
The intellectual debt is total and unacknowledged.

** 1971 --- Lampson's Access Control

Lampson's access control matrix (1971): subjects, objects, rights.
A cell containing "no write" IS a negative contract. The entire
history of OS security --- Multics rings, Unix permissions, SELinux
MAC --- is enforced negative contracts. StrongDM's eBPF enforcement
of "agents may not write to /etc/" is mandatory access control, solved
in Multics (1965). Cedar descends from XACML (2003), which descends
from Lampson. The lineage is clear; the citation is absent.

** 1975 --- Dijkstra's Guarded Commands

Dijkstra's guarded commands (CACM, 1975): =if B1 -> S1 [] B2 -> S2 fi=.
A command executes /only if/ its guard is true; no satisfied guard
means abort. Guards ARE negative contracts: they specify when execution
must not proceed. LangGraph's conditional edges are guarded commands
over a state graph. CrewAI's function-based guardrails are guards.

** 1978 --- Hoare's CSP and Refusal Sets

Hoare's CSP (1978, 1985 book) introduced /refusal sets/: events a
process refuses in a given state. The failures model represents a
process as (trace, refusal) pairs --- what it has done and what it
refuses next. This IS "negative contract with runtime enforcement,"
formalized in 1978 with a complete algebraic theory for composition,
refinement, and verification. FDR (since 1991) mechanically verifies
that implementations never perform refused events --- stronger than
any mechanism the companion report surveys.

** 1986 --- Meyer's Design by Contract

Meyer's Design by Contract (1986; Eiffel, 1988) is the most directly
relevant prior art and the most conspicuously absent. Preconditions,
postconditions, class invariants --- with /runtime checking/ that
raises exceptions on violation. This is /tested, enforced/ negative
contracts, in 1986.

The ABC paper's "hard invariants" are Meyer's class invariants. Its
"governance constraints" are preconditions. Its "recovery mechanisms"
are exception handlers. The intellectual debt is total.

Meyer also identified the composition rule: subclass preconditions may
be /weakened/, postconditions must be /strengthened/. For agent
delegation: a delegated agent may accept more inputs but must satisfy
at least the same output constraints. No surveyed framework addresses
this.

** 1987 --- Liskov Substitution Principle

Liskov and Wing's behavioral subtyping (1987, formalized 1994): a
subtype must honor its supertype's contracts. This IS contract
compliance for delegated agents. When agent A delegates to agent B,
behavioral subtyping requires B satisfies A's contracts. No framework
checks this. The companion report does not mention Liskov.

** 1994 --- Lamport's TLA+

TLA+ specifies safety ("bad things never happen") and liveness ("good
things eventually happen"). Safety IS a negative contract over temporal
traces: =Safety == [](~agent_deleted_tests)= --- the box operator []
means "always." TLC model-checks this over all reachable states. The
companion report's "Behavioral Drift Score" is a probabilistic
relaxation of what TLA+ safety properties provide deterministically.

* What the AI Community Actually Reinvented

Let us map the companion report's "innovations" to their prior art:

| Report Concept | Prior Art | Year | Gap |
|----------------------------+---------------------------+------+---------------------------------------------|
| Negative contracts | Deontic prohibition (F) | 1951 | 75 years |
| Preconditions/Postconditions | Hoare logic | 1969 | 57 years |
| Hard/soft invariants | Meyer's class invariants | 1986 | 40 years |
| Tool set restriction | Lampson's access matrix | 1971 | 55 years |
| Guarded execution | Dijkstra's guarded cmds | 1975 | 51 years |
| Refusal sets | Hoare's CSP | 1978 | 48 years |
| Graph topology constraints | Petri nets (1962), CSP | 1962 | 64 years |
| Runtime enforcement | DbC runtime checks | 1986 | 40 years |
| Behavioral drift score | TLA+ safety properties | 1994 | 32 years |

The median age of the "missing primitive" is 48 years.

* What IS Actually New (And Worth Studying)

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging what the agentic AI
context genuinely adds to the conversation:

** 1. Stochastic Agents Require Probabilistic Contracts

Classical formal methods assume deterministic systems. LLM agents are
stochastic: Hoare triples hold with /probability/, invariants are
/statistical/, refinement must account for distributional shift. The
ABC paper's (p, delta, k)-satisfaction is a genuine contribution ---
adapting classical contracts to stochastic execution. It deserves
credit AND citation to the theory it extends.

** 2. Natural Language Contracts Are a New Modality

No classical system uses natural language as the contract
specification language. CLAUDE.md files, system prompts, and agent
descriptions are contracts written in English and "enforced" by a
language model's interpretation. This introduces:

- Ambiguity that formal specifications exclude by construction
- Interpretation drift as the model's behavior changes across versions
- The "spirit vs letter" problem that Asimov explored in fiction

This is genuinely new territory. But calling it "contracts" without
acknowledging that the field has spent fifty years developing
/unambiguous/ contract languages is negligent.

** 3. Delegation Composition at Scale

"If A delegates to B, and B to C, does C satisfy A's contracts?" is
a behavioral subtyping problem (Liskov), but stochastic and
natural-language aspects make classical solutions insufficient.

** 4. The Runtime Cost Constraint

Classical model checking is exponential. The ABC paper's "<10ms
per-action enforcement" makes contract checking practical in
interactive loops. An engineering achievement, not a theoretical one
--- but engineering achievements matter.

* Where the Companion Report Goes Wrong

** Mistaking Rediscovery for Discovery

The report treats "negative contracts are emerging as a critical
primitive" as a finding. It is not a finding; it is a literature
review failure. Negative contracts are a /well-established/ primitive
with decades of theory, tooling, and practical deployment.

** Ignoring the Composition Problem

No framework surveyed addresses what happens when contracts compose.
If agent A must not delete tests, and agent A delegates to agent B,
must B also not delete tests? Classical contract theory (Meyer, Liskov)
answers yes, with formal rules for how contracts propagate through
delegation. The agentic AI community has no answer because it has not
read the question.

** Conflating Enforcement with Theory

The report's taxonomy (prose / post-hoc / structural / runtime /
formal) conflates specification, verification, and enforcement ---
three distinct concerns. Do you have the /right/ contracts? That
question is independent of how you enforce them.

** Missing the Frame Problem

The deepest issue: specifying everything an agent must NOT do is
combinatorially explosive (McCarthy and Hayes, 1969). "Must not delete
tests, must not corrupt the database, must not leak credentials, must
not introduce backdoors ..." is an infinite enumeration.

Classical AI solved this with the /closed-world assumption/: everything
not explicitly permitted is forbidden. StrongDM and LangGraph implement
this; the rest enumerate prohibitions, which is the frame problem.

The report should ask: open-world or closed-world default? This is
the single most important design decision in agent contracts, and
the companion report does not pose the question.

* Recommendations

1. *Read the literature.* Meyer's /OOSC/ ch. 11-12, Hoare's /CSP/
   ch. 3, Lamport's /Specifying Systems/ ch. 1-8, Liskov and Wing
   (1994). These are not obscure. The failure to cite them leads to
   inferior engineering.

2. *Default to closed-world.* Everything not explicitly permitted is
   forbidden (Saltzer and Schroeder, 1975). The companion report notes
   "only StrongDM and LangGraph make unrequested functionality
   impossible" --- that is the observation that only two frameworks
   implement closed-world. The rest try to enumerate prohibitions,
   which is the frame problem, unsolvable by enumeration.

3. *Use existing formal languages.* TLA+, Alloy, CSP, JML already
   express contracts. New DSLs (ContractSpec, AgentSpec) should
   demonstrate at least equal expressiveness, or they are regressions.

4. *Test composition.* The hard problem is not "does agent A satisfy
   contract C?" but "does the multi-agent system satisfy property P?"
   This is CSP refinement, Liskov substitutability, and TLA+
   compositional verification. Existing theory applies.

* Conclusion

The companion report's practical survey is valuable. Its theoretical
framing --- "negative contracts are emerging" --- is ahistorical.
The foundations are fifty to eighty years old, well-studied, and
equipped with formal tools the current generation has not adopted.

The missing primitive is not negative contracts. The missing primitive
is /the AI community reading the formal methods literature/.

Or, as Dijkstra might have put it: the question of whether agents can
be constrained by contracts is as relevant as the question of whether
submarines can swim.
