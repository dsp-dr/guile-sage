* Preamble

The companion report ("Comprehension Debt in AI-Assisted Codebases,"
20260414-comprehension-debt.org) surveys the 2026 discourse on
comprehension debt as if the phenomenon were recently discovered.  It is
not.  The report cites Osmani, Storey, Karpathy, and several arXiv
preprints --- all from 2025-2026 --- while largely ignoring five decades
of computer science, cognitive science, and epistemology that anticipated
every finding it presents.

This is not a rebuttal.  The comprehension debt problem is real, the
measurements are useful, and the recommendations are sound.  But the
discourse suffers from /ahistoricism/: by failing to connect to prior
art, it risks re-deriving known results, ignoring known failures, and
missing the deeper structural reasons these problems recur.

What follows is a map from each "new" claim to its intellectual
ancestors, and an argument that the recurrence itself is the most
important signal.

* Brooks: Essential Complexity Cannot Be Automated Away (1986)

Frederick Brooks' "No Silver Bullet --- Essence and Accident in Software
Engineering" draws a distinction that the comprehension debt literature
rediscovers without crediting:

#+BEGIN_QUOTE
The essence of a software entity is a construct of interlocking
concepts: data sets, relationships among data items, algorithms, and
invocations of functions.  This essence is abstract, in that the
conceptual construct is the same under many different representations.
It is nonetheless highly precise and richly detailed.
--- Brooks, 1986
#+END_QUOTE

Brooks argued that /essential/ complexity --- the irreducible difficulty
of the problem domain --- could never be eliminated by better tools.
Only /accidental/ complexity (the friction introduced by inadequate
notation, slow compilation, poor tooling) yields to engineering
improvement.

Comprehension debt /is/ essential complexity wearing a new hat.
Understanding why a system works the way it does, what trade-offs were
made, and how modules interact under unexpected conditions --- this is
the essence.  AI removes the accidental complexity of typing code.  It
does not touch the essential complexity of understanding it.  The 2026
literature's surprise at this fact suggests the field forgot Brooks.

The epistemic debt formula Ed(t) = Uf(t) - Cc(t) is a quantification of
what Brooks stated qualitatively: you can push functional utility (Uf)
arbitrarily high through tooling, but corrective competence (Cc) is
bounded by essential complexity that no tool eliminates.

* Parnas: Software Aging (1994)

David Parnas' "Software Aging" describes a phenomenon isomorphic to
comprehension debt, three decades before the term was coined:

#+BEGIN_QUOTE
Programs, like people, get old.  We can't prevent aging, but we can
understand its causes, take steps to limits its effects, temporarily
reverse some of the damage it has caused, and prepare for the day when
the software is no longer viable.
--- Parnas, 1994
#+END_QUOTE

Parnas identifies two causes of software aging: (1) failure to adapt to
a changing environment, and (2) changes made by people who do not
understand the original design.  The second cause /is/ comprehension
debt.  Every patch applied without understanding accelerates decay.

The 2026 discourse treats this as an AI-specific problem.  It is not.
AI merely increases the rate at which cause (2) occurs, because it
enables changes by entities (agents) that /cannot/ understand the
original design --- they can only pattern-match against it.

Parnas' proposed remedy --- "preventive medicine" through documentation,
design recovery, and planned redesign --- maps directly to the companion
report's recommendations for ADRs, theory rebuilds, and intent
documents.  The recommendation is correct; the novelty claim is not.

* Lehman: Laws of Software Evolution (1974-1996)

Manny Lehman's laws of software evolution, developed over two decades of
empirical study, predict comprehension debt as a mathematical
inevitability:

- *Law I (Continuing Change)*: A system must be continually adapted or
  it becomes progressively less satisfactory.
- *Law II (Increasing Complexity)*: As a system evolves, its complexity
  increases /unless work is done to maintain or reduce it/.
- *Law VI (Continuing Growth)*: Functional content must be continually
  increased to maintain user satisfaction.

Law II is the one that matters here.  Complexity increases unless
/active work/ is done to fight it.  Comprehension debt is what
accumulates when that work is not done.  The 2026 discourse presents
this as a discovery; Lehman presented it as a law, supported by
longitudinal data from IBM OS/360 and other systems.

AI accelerates Law VI (feature generation) while doing nothing to
address Law II (complexity management).  The "speed asymmetry" that
Osmani and Ronacher describe is Lehman's Law II meeting exponentially
faster Law VI.  The math was always going to produce this outcome.

* Naur: Programming as Theory Building (1985)

The companion report cites Storey citing Naur, but does not engage
directly with Peter Naur's original argument, which is more radical than
the 2026 literature acknowledges:

#+BEGIN_QUOTE
Programming properly should be regarded as an activity by which the
programmers form or achieve a certain kind of insight, a theory, of the
matters at hand.  This suggestion is in contrast to what appears to be a
more common notion, that programming should be regarded as a production
of a program and certain other texts.
--- Naur, 1985
#+END_QUOTE

Naur's claim is not merely that understanding matters alongside code.
His claim is that the /code is not the program/.  The program is the
theory in the programmer's mind.  The code is an artifact that can be
used to reconstruct the theory, but incompletely.

If Naur is right --- and forty years of maintenance nightmares suggest he
is --- then AI-generated code is not a "program" in any meaningful sense.
It is an artifact without a corresponding theory.  The comprehension debt
literature circles around this insight without quite arriving at it,
because arriving at it would require confronting an uncomfortable
conclusion: AI does not program.  It emits artifacts that look like the
output of programming.

* Polanyi: Tacit Knowledge (1966)

"We can know more than we can tell" (Polanyi, 1966).  Much programmer
understanding is /tacit/ --- intuitions about where bugs lurk, a sense
of which abstractions fit, an ability to predict change propagation ---
that resists formalization.

The companion report proposes quizzes, rationale probes, and explanation
gates.  These measure /explicit/ knowledge only.  An engineer might fail
a comprehension quiz yet debug brilliantly under pressure.  An LLM might
produce a "Relational" SOLO-taxonomy explanation without possessing any
understanding at all.  Every proposed comprehension metric has a built-in
blind spot that cannot be eliminated, only acknowledged.

* Dreyfus: The Skill Acquisition Model (1980)

The Dreyfus model (Novice -> Advanced Beginner -> Competent -> Proficient
-> Expert) distinguishes stages not by quantity of knowledge but by
/kind/ of engagement.  AI coding assistants operate at "Competent":
rule-following, pattern application, plan execution.  They cannot reach
"Proficient" (holistic situation recognition) or "Expert" (intuitive
grasp bypassing deliberation).

The Anthropic study's 17% comprehension drop is a Dreyfus effect:
developers are held at Novice/Advanced Beginner because the AI handles
the Competent stage for them.  They never build the pattern recognition
that comes from struggling through it.  The "Explanation Gate" works
because it forces passage through the Competent stage --- articulating
plans and rationale --- that AI delegation otherwise skips.

* Wittgenstein: The Beetle in the Box (1953)

Wittgenstein's thought experiment: everyone has a box containing
something called a "beetle," but no one can look in anyone else's box.
The word "beetle" functions in language regardless of what is (or is not)
inside.  Replace "beetle" with "understanding" and you have the deep
problem of comprehension measurement: no one can inspect another's
comprehension.  We observe only behavioral proxies --- quiz answers,
debugging speed, review quality.  The explanation is a /performance/, not
a window into cognition.  Measure comprehension; but maintain
epistemological humility about what the measurements mean.

* The Socratic Method and the Explanation Gate (399 BC)

The Explanation Gate --- requiring novices to explain causal logic before
integrating AI code --- is the Socratic method applied to code review.
The epistemic debt paper's 60% debt reduction at 3% utility cost is
/empirical validation of Socrates/.  But the 2,400-year lineage carries
implications the paper misses:

1. The method requires a /skilled questioner/.  Automating it with
   LLM-generated questions risks Socratic theater without substance.
2. It works through /discomfort/ --- what Socrates called /aporia/.
   Developers who find the gate annoying are experiencing it correctly.
3. It is fundamentally /interpersonal/.  Socrates responded dynamically
   to specific confusions of specific people.  A static gate is a
   degenerate case --- better than nothing, worse than dialogue.

* Sapir-Whorf, Kuhn, and Conway: Three Lenses the Report Omits

** Linguistic relativity (Sapir-Whorf)

Does the programming language shape what can be comprehended?  A Scheme
codebase makes recursion and closures natural; an AI trained on Python
generates Python idioms in Scheme syntax.  The result is /syntactically
valid/ but /idiomatically alien/.  This is a dimension of comprehension
debt the 2026 literature ignores: debt incurred when AI-generated code
does not match the cognitive idioms of the target language.

** Paradigm shifts (Kuhn, 1962)

If AI-assisted development is a paradigm shift, then measuring
comprehension debt against pre-AI baselines is like measuring automobile
reliability with horse-care metrics.  I do not believe we are in a
paradigm shift --- the evidence shows familiar patterns accelerated, not
transformed --- but the discourse should engage with the possibility.

** Conway's Law (1967)

The AI-human team has an asymmetric communication structure: human
specifies intent, AI generates code, human reviews partially.  No
peer-level design dialogue occurs.  Conway predicts the system will
mirror this asymmetry: high-level intent in specs, low-level
implementation without deliberation, and a structural gap between the
two.  That gap /is/ comprehension debt.  ADRs are an attempt to add the
missing communication channel --- but Conway warns it works only if
organizational incentives align.

* The Recurrence Is the Signal

The most important observation is one the 2026 discourse misses
entirely: /comprehension debt keeps being rediscovered/.

| Year | Author | Name | Core claim |
|------|--------|------|------------|
| 1966 | Polanyi | Tacit knowledge | Understanding resists formalization |
| 1974 | Lehman | Increasing complexity | Complexity grows unless actively fought |
| 1985 | Naur | Theory building | The program lives in the mind, not the code |
| 1986 | Brooks | Essential complexity | The hard part cannot be automated |
| 1994 | Parnas | Software aging | Uninformed changes accelerate decay |
| 2019 | Abramov | WET codebase | Abstraction can destroy comprehension |
| 2026 | Osmani | Comprehension debt | AI generates faster than we understand |
| 2026 | Storey | Cognitive debt | Shared theory erodes under AI pressure |
| 2026 | arXiv | Epistemic debt | Functional utility minus corrective competence |

The phenomenon is invariant across: programming paradigms (structured,
OO, functional), development methodologies (waterfall, agile, agentic),
tooling eras (assemblers, compilers, IDEs, AI), and organizational forms
(cathedral, bazaar, agentic).

This invariance suggests that comprehension debt is not a bug in the
development process.  It is a /fundamental property/ of the relationship
between human cognition and complex systems.  No tool, methodology, or
organizational structure eliminates it.  Each new era simply reveals a
new face of the same underlying constraint: human minds are finite, and
the systems they build are not.

* Implications for guile-sage

The companion report's self-assessment (comprehension score of 35-40%)
and recommendations are sound.  But the prior art suggests tempering
expectations:

1. *ADRs will help, but not enough.*  Parnas showed that documentation
   decays almost as fast as the understanding it captures.  ADRs must be
   actively maintained (budgeted time), not just written.

2. *The Explanation Gate will work, with caveats.*  It restores the
   Dreyfus Competent stage and invokes the Socratic method.  But
   Wittgenstein warns: articulate explanation is not proof of
   understanding.  The gate catches gross ignorance, not subtle
   misunderstanding.

3. *Comprehension metrics will always be proxies.*  Polanyi's tacit
   knowledge means no quiz, gate, or rubric can fully capture
   understanding.  Use the metrics; do not worship them.

4. *The problem will not be solved, only managed.*  Brooks, Lehman, and
   Parnas all point to the same conclusion: essential complexity is
   permanent.  The goal is not zero comprehension debt.  The goal is
   keeping debt at a level where the team can still diagnose and fix
   failures.

5. *Language-idiomatic code matters.*  The Sapir-Whorf connection
   suggests that AI-generated Guile code should be reviewed not just for
   correctness but for /idiomatic fit/.  Code that works but reads like
   translated Python accumulates comprehension debt specific to the
   Scheme community.

6. *Observe the system, not just the code.*  Majors is right, and
   guile-sage already has telemetry.  The next step is connecting
   runtime behavior (OTLP metrics) to comprehension indicators (time
   spent debugging, frequency of "why" questions in development logs).

* Conclusion

The 2026 discourse is valuable for quantifying what was previously
qualitative.  That is genuine progress.  But the failure to cite prior
art creates two risks: (1) re-deriving known results instead of building
on them, and (2) missing the structural explanation for /why/ these
problems recur across every era and methodology.

The field does not need a new vocabulary for an old problem.  It needs to
read its own history and build on it.
