#+TITLE: Prior Art Review: "Security as Proof of Work" Is Not New
#+DATE: 2026-04-14
#+AUTHOR: Prof. Marcus "Spark" Wellington
#+OPTIONS: toc:3 num:t

* Executive Summary

The companion report (=20260414-security-economics.org=) presents Drew
Breunig's "proof-of-work" framing of AI-driven security as a novel
insight. It is not. Every core claim in Breunig's thesis has
well-established prior art in security economics, game theory, and
software engineering literature spanning 1934--2008. What /is/ new is
the substrate (LLM tokens instead of analyst-hours), but the
underlying economic dynamics --- attacker-defender asymmetry,
diminishing returns on security investment, the public-goods problem
of open-source hardening --- were formalized decades ago.

This review identifies the prior art, assesses where the companion
report overclaims novelty, and raises structural concerns the report
fails to address: risk homeostasis, moral hazard, and the tragedy of
the commons in token-funded security.

* Prior Art: What Breunig Rediscovered

** Anderson (2001): Why Information Security Is Hard

Ross Anderson's "Why Information Security Is Hard --- An Economic
Perspective" (ACSAC 2001) established that security failures are
primarily /economic/, not technical: misaligned incentives, not
missing technology, explain most failures. Breunig's claim that
security is now a "spending game" rather than a "cleverness game"
restates Anderson's core thesis with "tokens" substituted for
"dollars." Anderson said it first and said it with formal models.

** Gordon-Loeb Model (2002): There IS a Maximum ROI

Lawrence Gordon and Martin Loeb's "The Economics of Information
Security Investment" (ACM TISSEC, 2002) proved a result the
companion report conspicuously ignores: *the optimal security
investment is bounded*. Specifically:

- A firm should never invest more than 36.8% (1/e) of the expected
  loss from a breach
- Additional investment beyond this point yields /negative/ marginal
  returns
- The optimal investment is a function of the vulnerability's
  probability and the potential loss, not an unbounded "spend more
  tokens" directive

The companion report's recommendation to "outspend the attacker"
(Section 7.1) directly contradicts Gordon-Loeb. If an attacker can
probe sage for $50, the report recommends spending $150+. But
Gordon-Loeb tells us to ask: what is the /expected loss/ from a
successful attack on sage? For a local Scheme REPL with no
production deployment, that expected loss may be close to zero ---
making even $28/release an overinvestment.

The report's proposed v1.0 hardening budget of $120 should be
justified against expected loss, not against attacker expenditure.
These are different quantities with different optima.

** Schneier (2000): Security Is a Process, Not a Product

Bruce Schneier's "The Process of Security" (2000) anticipated the
companion report's "hybrid model" by 26 years. The report's
per-commit scanning, per-release deep audits, and annual external
reviews are Schneier's layered process model with token budgets
attached. The structure is sound precisely /because/ it follows
Schneier's established pattern, not because Breunig invented it.

** Von Stackelberg (1934): Attacker-Defender Games

Heinrich von Stackelberg formalized leader-follower games in 1934.
Security is a textbook Stackelberg game: the defender commits to a
posture, the attacker observes and optimizes in response.

Breunig's "spend more tokens than attackers" assumes the attacker's
budget is fixed and observable. In practice, attackers /adapt/.
Spending 3x the attacker's budget tells them to spend 4x --- or to
shift surfaces entirely (social engineering, supply chain, insider
threat). The correct formulation is to invest up to the point where
the attacker's /marginal cost of attack/ exceeds the /marginal
value of a successful breach/ --- a calculation that depends on
asset value, not on the attacker's current token budget.

** Linus's Law, Updated: From Eyeballs to Tokens

Raymond's "given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow" (1997) is
the direct ancestor. The companion report implicitly updates it to
"given enough tokens, all vulnerabilities are discoverable." But
this inherits the original's known weaknesses:

- *Empirically fragile.* Heartbleed persisted in OpenSSL for two
  years; Shellshock hid in Bash for 25. Millions of eyeballs
  missed both.
- *Tokens are not eyeballs.* LLMs have systematic blind spots.
  More tokens with the same blind spots means more of the same
  coverage, not deeper coverage.
- *The right analogy is "more of the same eyeball."* Scaling
  inference compute amplifies one model's reasoning patterns; it
  does not diversify perspective.

** Ozment & Schechter (2006): Vulnerability Density Decreases

Andy Ozment and Stuart Schechter ("Milk or Wine: Does Software
Security Improve with Age?", USENIX Security 2006) showed that
vulnerability density in mature codebases decreases over time --- but
never reaches zero.

This result has two implications the companion report misses:

1. *sage's existing test suite may already capture most of the
   low-hanging fruit.* With 42+ security tests, 6,400+ PBT trials,
   and a 0.82:1 test-to-code ratio, the marginal vulnerability
   found by the next 35M tokens may be very low.

2. *The remaining vulnerabilities are the hard ones.* Ozment &
   Schechter showed that surviving bugs tend to be architectural,
   not superficial. LLM scanning is strongest against pattern-matched
   vulnerability classes (injection, traversal) and weakest against
   novel architectural issues --- precisely the class that persists.

** Bitcoin's Proof of Work (Nakamoto 2008): A Flawed Analogy

Breunig's explicit analogy to Bitcoin's proof of work is colorful but
economically misleading:

| Property                | Bitcoin PoW                          | "Security PoW"                    |
|-------------------------+--------------------------------------+-----------------------------------|
| What is proved          | Computational work was done          | Vulnerabilities were looked for   |
| Verifiability           | Trivially verifiable (hash check)    | Not verifiable (absence of bugs?) |
| Fungibility             | One hash = another hash              | Not all scans are equal           |
| Consensus               | Network agrees on chain state        | No consensus mechanism            |
| Sybil resistance        | Yes (by design)                      | N/A                               |
| Difficulty adjustment   | Automatic (every 2016 blocks)        | None proposed                     |
| Finality                | Probabilistic (6 confirmations)      | Never (new vulns emerge)          |

The core problem: Bitcoin's proof of work /proves/ something (that
computational resources were expended on a specific block). Security
token spending proves nothing about the /completeness/ of the scan.
You can spend 150M tokens and miss a critical vulnerability that a
5-minute human review would catch. The analogy conflates input
(tokens spent) with output (security achieved).

* Structural Concerns the Report Ignores

** Risk Homeostasis (Wilde 1982)

Gerald Wilde (1982) showed that when people /feel/ safer, they
compensate by taking more risks. Seatbelts led to faster driving.

Applied here: if teams believe 35M-token scans have "hardened"
their release, they may write riskier code, skip manual review, or
expand the attack surface. The companion report proposes hardening
as a "release gate" without addressing this behavioral feedback
loop. A gate that teams learn to treat as a rubber stamp provides
false assurance.

** Moral Hazard

If scanning is "effectively free" (local inference), the marginal
cost of introducing a vulnerability also becomes "effectively free
to fix." This may /increase/ the vulnerability introduction rate,
creating a treadmill. The insurance industry discovered centuries
ago that fully insured parties take more risks. The report should
specify what "deductible" (mandatory human sign-off, manual review
requirements) prevents moral hazard.

** Tragedy of the Commons: Who Pays for OSS Tokens?

The report claims OSS hardening costs are "amortized across users."
This is Hardin's tragedy of the commons (1968) in new clothes. OSS
security is a public good; public goods are underproduced because
actors free-ride. Who pays? Maintainers are burned out (Heartbleed,
xz backdoor). Companies free-ride. Governments are slow.
Foundations are underfunded. Tokens do not solve the incentive
structure --- they just change the denomination.

** Akerlof's Market for Lemons (1970)

Akerlof showed information asymmetry degrades market quality.
Buyers cannot distinguish secure software from insecure software;
the market does not reward security investment. The companion
report proposes token-spending thresholds as a release gate --- but
"we spent 35M tokens scanning" is an /input/ metric, not an
/output/ metric. A project that spent 35M tokens and found nothing
might be secure or might have a blind scanner. Without verifiable
output signals (formal verification, standardized certification),
token-spending metrics risk becoming security theater.

* What IS Genuinely New

To be fair, the report surfaces genuinely new empirical data:

1. *AISI's TLO cost data* ($80/attempt Opus 4.6, $12,500 Mythos)
   --- the first public figures for AI-powered attack economics.
2. *Log-linear offensive scaling with tokens* --- prior work assumed
   human-linear scaling; log-linear with compute is a new regime.
3. *LiteLLM supply chain incident* --- a valuable multi-hop case
   study in the LLM dependency ecosystem.
4. *Trail of Bits' 13x productivity data* --- first empirical basis
   for defensive scanning ROI.
5. *Local inference 6,000x cost advantage* --- defenders with local
   compute have a genuine structural edge over API-only attackers.

These data points are valuable. The framing around them is not.

* Recommendations

** 1. Ground the Analysis in Gordon-Loeb

Replace "outspend the attacker" with "invest up to Gordon-Loeb
optimal." This requires estimating the expected loss from a
successful attack on sage --- a number the report never calculates.
For a local development tool with no production deployment, this
number may be very small.

** 2. Address the Behavioral Feedback Loop

Any hardening regime must account for risk homeostasis. Proposed
mitigations:

- Mandatory human review of all unsafe-tool-related code changes,
  even after automated scanning
- Periodic "scan the scanner" exercises where human auditors check
  for systematic blind spots in the AI scanning
- Tracking not just vulnerabilities found but vulnerabilities
  /introduced/ per release, to detect moral hazard effects

** 3. Use Output Metrics, Not Input Metrics

"Tokens spent on hardening" is an input metric. Useful output
metrics include:

- Vulnerabilities found per release (trend over time)
- Time-to-detection for injected synthetic vulnerabilities
- Coverage metrics (which OWASP categories were actually tested?)
- False positive / false negative rates for the scanning pipeline

** 4. Acknowledge the Commons Problem

If OSS projects should adopt token-funded hardening, who funds the
tokens? Possible models: levies on commercial users (Tidelift),
government grants (EU FOSSA), foundation infrastructure (OpenSSF),
or cloud providers scanning dependencies as a loss-leader. Simply
stating "amortize across the community" is not a funding model.

** 5. Diversify Beyond Token Scaling

"Spend more tokens" is monoculture thinking. A robust program needs
formal methods (sage's Scheme is amenable to property-based
verification), architecture review by humans who understand Guile's
continuation-based control flow, diverse scanning tools (not one
vendor's model), and red team exercises with human adversaries.

* Conclusion

Breunig's "proof-of-work" framing is a catchy metaphor stretched
past its breaking point. Anderson, Gordon, Loeb, and Schneier
formalized these economics before the first Bitcoin block was mined.
Von Stackelberg modeled attacker-defender games when Turing was
still an undergraduate. The public-goods problem predates LLMs by
decades.

The companion report's empirical data (AISI, Trail of Bits) is
genuinely useful. Its practical recommendations are sound. But drop
the proof-of-work metaphor and ground investment decisions in
Gordon-Loeb, not in cryptocurrency analogies.

Spend your tokens wisely. Just do not pretend you invented the idea
of spending money on security.
